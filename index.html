My account of the maintenance performed on my 1998 M3/4/5. Yes, it has four doors. No, it's not a "family car."

In 1995, after retiring the E34 M5, BMW faced a void in their high-performance executive lineup. To bridge the gap between the E34 M5 and the next M5 (which would only start in late 1998), BMW needed something capable of filling the E34's shoes while restoring the reputation of both the M3 and the ///M division.

Despite heavy investment in the US during the 1980s, the E30 M3 flopped. Consequently, BMW detuned the North American E36 M3 so that if it failed to capture the market like the E30 M3, it wouldn't set them back as much as the E30 did.

As a result, the suits in Munich gave the E36 M3 two extra doors and a heavy dose of corporate plausible deniability. To their surprise, the E36 M3 did exceedingly well, and so did the succeeding generations of the M3 that followed the E36.
