Spark Plug R&R

March 12, 2026  ·  166,362 miles

I'm not a BMW technician. This article is provided freely for entertainment purposes only and is not intended to be instructional in nature. Use of the information in this article is entirely at your own risk.

Swapped out the old set of NGK BKR6EQUP (Laser Platinum) and replaced with the same type. The S52 was in need of new spark plugs — during cold starts I would experience symptoms of misfiring. Though spark plugs may not have been the sole culprit, it's always good to do some preventative maintenance before it turns into a headache.

In the end, the S52 idles noticeably smoother than before and no longer sounds like it woke up with a sore throat on a cold start. Job well done.

Prerequisites

Replacing spark plugs is one of the most rudimentary jobs any home mechanic can tackle. As someone who had never touched the inside of an engine bay before this, I can retrospectively confirm what the internet says: this job shouldn't be feared by the DIYer.

Ratchets
  • 3/8" ratchet — for removing valve cover nuts and coil pack bolts.
  • 1/2" ratchet — optional, but recommended for extracting the plugs. Less force required than a 3/8".
  • Torque wrench — must reach 18 ft-lbs comfortably (not at either extreme of the range). 18 ft-lbs is the torque spec for all six plugs.
Sockets & Extensions
  • 10mm socket
  • 6" extension — for 3/8" and 1/2" drives.
  • 5/8" spark plug socket — get the magnetic type so the plug doesn't drop back into the well on removal.
Parts
  • 6x NGK BKR6EQUP Laser Platinum spark plugs (or your preferred OEM+ equivalent).

Procedure

  1. Position your nail on the underside of the plastic valve cover nut cap. Flick it off with your other hand placed on top to stop it from flying. Repeat for the second cap.
  2. Using a 10mm socket, unscrew the two nuts.
  3. Unscrew the oil cap. Keep nothing near the open reservoir.
  4. Remove the valve cover, then screw the oil cap back on immediately.
  5. You should now see a neat row of six ignition coil packs connected by metal snap-on locks.
  6. Using the 10mm again, turn counterclockwise on each coil pack bolt. Note the orientation of the electrical ground strap on cylinders 1 and 6 — the thin strap sits above the coil.
  7. Pull up on the two metal locks with two fingers. The coils will jump up, making removal easy.
  8. You'll now see a deep well with the tip of the spark plug at the bottom. With the extension attached to your 3/8" or 1/2" and the 5/8" spark plug socket, reach down and turn counterclockwise. Keep the ratchet flat — parallel to the engine head.
  9. Apply consistent, even pressure. If the plug still won't budge, a small shot of WD-40 can help break it loose.
  10. Once the old plug is out, slide the new one in threads-first.
  11. Remove the ratchet from the extension and hand-tighten the plug first. Skipping this step risks cross-threading — which means rebuilding the head.
  12. Once the plug is snug by hand, torque to 18 ft-lbs.
  13. Repeat for all six cylinders.
  14. Reinstall coils, nuts, and valve cover in reverse order.
