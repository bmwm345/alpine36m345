Spark Plug R&R

March 12, 2026  ·  166,362 miles

I'm not a BMW technician. This article is provided freely for entertainment purposes only and is not intended to be instructional in nature. Use of the information here is entirely at your own risk.

Swapped out the old set of NGK BKR6EQUP (Laser Platinum) and replaced with the same type. The S52 was showing symptoms of misfiring on cold starts — though spark plugs may not have been the sole culprit, preventative maintenance made sense before it became a real headache.

In the end, the S52 idles noticeably smoother and no longer sounds like it woke up with a sore throat on a cold start. Job well done.

Prerequisites

Replacing spark plugs is one of the most rudimentary jobs a home mechanic can take on. Having never touched an engine bay before this, I can confirm: don't fear it.

Ratchets
  • 3/8" ratchet — valve cover nuts and coil pack bolts.
  • 1/2" ratchet — optional but recommended for plug extraction. Easier on the hands.
  • Torque wrench — must reach 18 ft-lbs comfortably. That's the spec for all six plugs.
Sockets & Extensions
  • 10mm socket
  • 6" extension — for both 3/8" and 1/2" drives.
  • 5/8" spark plug socket — magnetic type strongly recommended.
Parts
  • 6x NGK BKR6EQUP Laser Platinum spark plugs.

Procedure

  1. Position your nail on the underside of the plastic valve cover nut cap and flick it off, hand on top to stop it flying. Repeat for the second cap.
  2. Using a 10mm socket, unscrew the two nuts.
  3. Unscrew the oil cap. Keep nothing near the open reservoir.
  4. Remove the valve cover, then screw the oil cap back on immediately.
  5. You'll see a row of six ignition coil packs connected by metal snap-on locks.
  6. Using the 10mm, turn counterclockwise on each coil pack bolt. Note the orientation of the ground strap on cylinders 1 and 6 — the thin strap sits above the coil.
  7. Pull up on the two metal locks with two fingers. The coils will jump up for easy removal.
  8. Deep well ahead — spark plug tip visible at the bottom. Extension on your ratchet, 5/8" plug socket attached, reach down and turn counterclockwise. Keep the ratchet flat, parallel to the engine head.
  9. Apply consistent, even pressure. If the plug won't budge, a short hit of WD-40 helps break it loose.
  10. Old plug out, new one in — threads first.
  11. Remove the ratchet from the extension and hand-tighten first. Skipping this risks cross-threading, which means rebuilding the head.
  12. Once snug by hand, torque to 18 ft-lbs.
  13. Repeat for all six cylinders.
  14. Reinstall coils, nuts, and valve cover in reverse order.
